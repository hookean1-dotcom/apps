Compare the four designs

Galilean refractor

Galileo, 1609
  • Key elements: convex objective lens, concave eyepiece lens placed before the focus
  • Gives an upright image
  • Very narrow field of view — hard to find things
  • Strong chromatic aberration
  • 11.24: even so, Galileo saw lunar mountains, Jupiter's four moons, the phases of Venus and countless stars in the Milky Way — evidence for a Sun-centred model

Keplerian refractor

Kepler, 1611
  • Key elements: convex objective lens, convex eyepiece lens placed after the focus
  • Much wider field of view than the Galilean
  • Image is inverted — fine for astronomy
  • Still suffers chromatic aberration; large lenses sag under their own weight and must be supported at the edges

Newtonian reflector

Newton, 1668
  • Key elements: concave primary mirror, flat secondary mirror at 45°, eyepiece in the side of the tube
  • Mirrors reflect all colours identically — no chromatic aberration (11.25a)
  • Mirrors can be supported across the whole back surface, so very large apertures are possible (11.25c)
  • Cheap per centimetre of aperture

Cassegrain reflector

Cassegrain, 1672
  • Key elements: concave primary mirror with a central hole, convex secondary mirror, eyepiece behind the primary
  • Folds a very long focal length into a short tube (11.25b)
  • Compact and portable for the aperture
  • The design used by most large professional telescopes, often with multiple or segmented mirrors (11.25d)

Key ideas

    
Why aperture beats magnification
Light grasp goes with the area of the objective, so doubling the diameter collects four times the light (11.19). Magnification only spreads that light out — it adds no new detail. A big, low-power telescope beats a small, high-power one every time.

    
Why the eye is so limited (11.14)
A dark-adapted pupil is only about 7 mm across and the retina cannot store light the way a camera sensor can. So the naked eye reaches roughly magnitude 6 and cannot resolve detail finer than about 1 arcminute.

    
Resolution and wavelength (11.22)
Resolution improves in proportion to the diameter of the objective, and gets worse at longer wavelengths. Switch the band to radio and watch what happens — this is why radio dishes must be enormous (13.25) and why astronomers link them into arrays (13.26).

    
Chromatic aberration (11.25a)
A lens refracts blue light more strongly than red, so the colours focus at slightly different points and bright objects gain a coloured fringe. A mirror reflects every colour identically, so reflectors avoid the problem entirely.

    
Exit pupil
The width of the beam of light leaving the eyepiece. Below about 0.5 mm the image is dim and mushy; above about 7 mm the extra light misses your pupil and is wasted. This sets the sensible magnification range.
