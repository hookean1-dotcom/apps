The two models

What is retrograde motion? (5.5)

Usually a planet drifts slowly eastward against the background stars. For a few weeks around opposition it appears to stop, move westward (retrograde), stop again, then resume eastward — tracing a loop or zig-zag.

The heliocentric explanation

Earth and the planet both orbit the Sun. Earth, on a faster inner orbit, overtakes the outer planet near opposition. Just as a fast car overtaking a slower one makes the slower car seem to move backwards, the planet appears to slip westwards. It is an illusion of our own motion.

Ptolemy's geocentric model (7.3)

With the Earth fixed at the centre, a planet moving on a single circle could never go backwards. Ptolemy kept the Earth central but had each planet ride a small circle, the epicycle, whose centre moved around a big circle, the deferent.

Why the epicycle works (7.4)

As the planet loops around its epicycle, it periodically moves backwards against the stars — reproducing retrograde motion while keeping Earth at the centre. It is a genuinely clever fix, and it predicted planetary positions well for 1400 years.

The hidden clue

Turn on the Sun link. In Ptolemy's model the line from the epicycle centre to the planet stays exactly parallel to the Earth–Sun line — an unexplained "coincidence." Copernicus realised it is no coincidence: the epicycle is secretly a copy of Earth's own orbit.
