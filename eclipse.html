Key ideas (3.10)

What causes a solar eclipse?

At new Moon the Moon passes between the Earth and Sun. If the alignment is close enough, the Moon's shadow falls on the Earth and blocks the Sun. Seen from inside the dark umbra it is total; from the lighter penumbra it is partial.

What causes a lunar eclipse?

At full Moon the Earth passes between the Sun and Moon, and the Moon moves into the Earth's shadow. It is visible from the entire night side of Earth, unlike a solar eclipse.

Why is a total lunar eclipse red?

Sunlight is refracted and scattered by the Earth's atmosphere into the umbra. Blue light is scattered away and red light bent inward, so the Moon glows a dim coppery red — the light of every sunrise and sunset on Earth at once.

Total vs annular (3.8)

The Moon's distance varies. Near perigee it looks slightly larger than the Sun and can cover it completely — total. Near apogee it looks slightly smaller, leaving a bright ring — annular.

Why not every month?

The Moon's orbit is tilted ~5°, so usually the new or full Moon is above or below the exact line-up. Eclipses only occur near the nodes (3.10).
