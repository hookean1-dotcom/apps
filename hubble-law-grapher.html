Key ideas

    
Hubble's law (16.5)
v = H₀ d: the faster a galaxy recedes (v), the further away it is (d). Plotting v against d gives a straight line through the origin, and its gradient is the Hubble constant H₀.

    
Age of the Universe (16.6)
If every galaxy has been moving at constant speed since the Big Bang, the time taken to reach distance d is d/v = 1/H₀. So age ≈ 1/H₀ — the same for every galaxy, which is why they all lie on one line.

    
Units matter
H₀ is in km/s per Mpc. To get an age you must convert: 1 Mpc = 3.09×10¹⁹ km, and 1 year = 3.16×10⁷ s. This turns 1/H₀ into a time. A handy shortcut: age ≈ 978/H₀ billion years.

    
Why Hubble's first value was wrong
In 1929 Hubble measured H₀ ≈ 500, giving an age of only 2 billion years — younger than the rocks on Earth! His galaxy distances were badly underestimated. Better distances later lowered H₀ to about 70, giving ~14 billion years.

    
Size of the Universe
The Hubble radius c/H₀ (~14 billion light-years) is roughly how far light could travel in the age of the Universe — a rough measure of the size of the observable Universe.
