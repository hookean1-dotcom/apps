The three methods (12.4)

① Transit method

When a planet passes in front of its star, it blocks a little light and the star dims. The depth of the dip = (Rplanet/Rstar)², giving the planet's size; the time between dips gives the period. Only works if the orbit is nearly edge-on so the planet crosses the disc. Most exoplanets were found this way (Kepler, TESS).

② Radial velocity

The planet and star both orbit their common centre of mass, so the star wobbles slightly towards and away from us. This Doppler-shifts its spectral lines — blue as it approaches, red as it recedes. The size of the shift gives the planet's mass (strictly, a minimum mass, M sin i). Works best for massive, close-in planets on edge-on orbits.

③ Astrometry

The star's wobble also moves its position slightly on the sky, tracing a tiny ellipse. Measuring this gives the planet's mass and orbit. The wobble is largest for massive planets in wide orbits around nearby stars, and — unlike RV — works even for face-on orbits. The shifts are tiny (micro-arcseconds), so it needs spacecraft like Gaia.

Why they complement each other

Transit and RV need edge-on orbits and favour close-in planets; astrometry favours wide orbits and works face-on. Combining transit (size) with RV (mass) gives the planet's density — and whether it is rocky or gaseous.
