Key ideas

What is the Equation of Time? (4.4)

EoT = Apparent Solar Time − Mean Solar Time. Apparent time is what a sundial shows (the real Sun); mean time is what a clock shows (a smoothly-moving "mean Sun"). The two disagree by up to about 16 minutes.

Cause 1 — axial tilt (4.6)

The real Sun moves along the ecliptic, but time is kept by its motion along the celestial equator. Because the ecliptic is tilted 23.4°, projecting one onto the other speeds the Sun up near the solstices and slows it near the equinoxes — a wave that repeats twice a year.

Cause 2 — elliptical orbit (4.6)

Earth moves fastest at perihelion (early January) and slowest at aphelion (early July), so the Sun appears to move unevenly along the ecliptic. This gives a wave that repeats once a year.

Why the annual variation? (4.5)

Adding a twice-yearly wave to a once-yearly wave gives the familiar double-humped curve, crossing zero four times and reaching about +16 min in early November and −14 min in mid-February.

What is the analemma?

Plotting the Sun's declination against the Equation of Time over a year traces a figure-8. It is why the "noon" Sun photographed all year makes a stretched 8 in the sky, and why it appears on some sundials and globes.
