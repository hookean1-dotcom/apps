Edexcel GCSE Astronomy 1AS0 · spec points 6.7 – 6.16 · converting between equatorial (RA/Dec) and horizon (Alt/Az) coordinates

1 · Where are you?

Your latitude fixes how the sky is tilted. The celestial pole always sits at an altitude equal to your latitude.

2 · When is it?

LST is simply the RA that is on your meridian right now. So when LST = the object's RA, the object is crossing the meridian — that is its culmination.

        Why these formulae?
        
The two coordinate systems describe the same sky, but the equatorial system is fixed to the stars while the horizon system is fixed to you. Your latitude and the hour angle are what connect them, which is exactly what spec point 6.10 asks for.

        
You are not required to recall these formulae for the exam — but working through them makes the link between the two systems concrete, and the meridian results (culmination altitude, circumpolarity) are examinable.

Key terms (6.7 & 6.13)

      
Celestial sphere
An imaginary sphere of very large radius, centred on the Earth, onto which all celestial objects appear to be projected.

      
Celestial poles & equator
The points and circle you get by extending the Earth's axis and equator out onto the celestial sphere. The NCP sits at altitude = your latitude, due north. Note that Polaris is not exactly at the NCP — it sits about 0.7° away, which is why the app shows the two separately.

      
Meridian
The great circle running from due north, through the zenith, to due south. An object is at its highest as it crosses it.

      
Zenith
The point directly overhead, altitude 90°.

      
Culmination
The moment an object crosses the observer's meridian at upper transit — its greatest altitude of the day.

      
Circumpolar
A star that never sets from your latitude, because even at lower transit it stays above the horizon. Requires δ ≥ 90° − φ in the northern hemisphere.

      
Hour angle
How far west of the meridian an object has travelled, measured in hours. HA = LST − RA. HA = 0 means the object is on the meridian.

      
Cardinal points
North, east, south and west on the horizon — azimuth 0°, 90°, 180° and 270°.
